Research Question

Which energetic ions remain confined?

WHAM uses a magnetic-mirror geometry to confine plasma between regions of stronger magnetic field. Particles moving primarily along the field can stream through the mirrors, while particles with sufficient perpendicular velocity reflect and remain trapped. At higher energy, finite gyro-orbit size and the device geometry also open possible radial-loss pathways.

The project grew from tracking individual trajectories into a reusable framework that samples initial position, velocity, and gyro phase. Each particle is then classified as confined, axially escaped, past a limiter-related flux surface, or across a harder fast-ion radial boundary.

The work is directly connected to questions our WHAM collaborators care about, including the confinement of energetic deuterium produced by neutral-beam injection and the eventual calculation of charge-exchange losses.

Model Architecture

From COMSOL fields to orbit classification

The field evaluator loads WHAM's axisymmetric radial and axial magnetic-field components, interpolates them at each particle location, and converts the result into Cartesian coordinates. A Boris pusher advances the full gyromotion under the Lorentz force while tracking position, velocity, energy, mirror bounces, and boundary crossings.

The same orbit engine is reused across position scans, pitch-angle studies, velocity- space sweeps, and weighted distribution calculations. This makes it possible to test physical conclusions across large populations rather than relying on a few visually appealing trajectories.

Field evaluation

Interpolate Br and Bz on the WHAM grid and recover the Cartesian field at arbitrary particle positions.

Orbit integration

Advance full particle gyromotion with the Boris algorithm rather than relying only on a guiding-center approximation.

Classification

Separate confined particles from axial escape, hard radial loss, limiter-surface crossing, and unresolved cases.

Population analysis

Repeat across v⊥, v∥, position, and gyro phase, then weight the outcomes with a physical source distribution.

Verification

Building a trustworthy baseline

Before interpreting large parameter scans, I checked the integrator against the expected magnetic-mirror loss cone and repeated runs with smaller timesteps and longer durations. The escaped population concentrates near field-aligned motion, while the trapped/passing boundary follows the theoretical mirror trend.

Energy is conserved to approximately one part in 1013 in the validated collisionless runs. That matters because small numerical heating or cooling can shift the loss boundary over a long integration and create a physically plausible but numerically false result.

Boundary Modeling

The radial boundary changes the conclusion

One of the most important results so far was a modeling lesson rather than a single percentage: radial loss is highly sensitive to how the physical device boundary is represented. A simple cylindrical hard wall classified 19% of a pilot population as radially lost. When the boundary was tied to the relevant WHAM limiter flux surface, the inferred loss fell below 1% in the same sweep.

A geometric radius can count a finite gyro-orbit excursion as a true loss even when the guiding trajectory remains associated with the confined region. The current classifier therefore records limiter crossing and hard radial loss separately instead of collapsing every radial excursion into one outcome.

Interpretation: the boundary definition is part of the physical model, not a cosmetic post-processing choice.

Distribution Functions

From equally weighted scans to physical populations

A raw classifier answers what happens to each sampled initial condition, but not how important that condition is in a real population. I therefore map the outcomes onto normalized F(v⊥,v∥) distributions so the confined and lost components are weighted by the source rather than treating every velocity bin equally.

These maps make the loss cone, confined core, and lower-weight radial-loss regions visible in the same coordinate system. They also provide a foundation for following how an initially isotropic distribution evolves once collisions and additional loss physics are included.

Current Direction

Adding the next layer of physics

The current workflow produces position- and velocity-space maps for confined, axially lost, radially lost, and limiter-crossing populations. The next development step is to introduce collisions so that pitch-angle scattering can repopulate the loss cone over time rather than leaving each orbit permanently collisionless.

I am also extending the model toward the 20–25 keV deuterium ions created by WHAM's neutral-beam system. Charge exchange, limiter geometry, and a self-consistent electrostatic potential are longer-term directions once the collision operator and energetic source are validated.

Near term

  • Validate pitch-angle scattering and collision timescales
  • Finalize weighted F(v⊥,v∥) evolution
  • Compare limiter and fast-ion flux boundaries

Longer term

  • Model energetic neutral-beam deuterium
  • Calculate charge-exchange loss directions and magnitudes
  • Evaluate electric-field and Pastukhov-potential effects
Takeaway

I am an engineer first and naturally gravitate toward hardware, but this project has added computational depth that I value. More importantly, the computation is not detached from the device: it uses WHAM's actual fields and addresses questions that matter to an operating fusion experiment.

The work has required me to connect plasma theory, numerical methods, software design, validation, and physical interpretation in one sustained project.
