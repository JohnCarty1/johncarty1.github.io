Biomimetic Robotics

Nauplius-Inspired Swimming Robot

October 2024 – January 2026

Working with Nathan Brewer under Professor Carr Everbach, I helped translate the propulsion strategy of a barnacle nauplius into a controllable mechanical system. The final prototype coordinated three limb pairs, enclosed its electronics in a waterproof biologically informed housing, and could be activated remotely by light.

RoleMechanical design & integration

Mechanism development, CAD, prototyping, fabrication, enclosure design, and testing.

SystemThree-pair swimming mechanism

Demonstrated phase-controlled limb motion, waterproof packaging, and light activation.

ToolsFusion 360 + fabrication

Motivation

Why biomimetic motion?

The barnacle nauplius uses an asymmetric, phased swimming stroke to move through a highly viscous environment near the Stokes-flow regime. At that scale, inertia contributes very little, so the order and timing of each limb motion strongly affect whether the organism produces net propulsion.

In collaboration with researchers at the University of Washington School of Oceanography, our goal was to create a physical model that reproduced the organism's appendage motion closely enough to study its swimming dynamics under controlled conditions.

The project also connected naturally to my interest in systems for extreme environments. Propulsion in dense or constrained fluids may matter in settings such as subsurface oceans, where conventional approaches do not translate cleanly.

Mechanism

One motor, six coordinated limbs

A single motor input is transmitted through a custom gear train and linkage system to generate coordinated, periodic motion across three pairs of limbs. The central design challenge was reproducing both the angular sweep of each limb and the phase offset between pairs.

Pivot locations, linkage lengths, and gear timing were iterated together. The design also had to remain compact enough to preserve the approximate appendage spacing and overall proportions of the biological system.

Mechanical Validation

First functioning mechanism

This test was the first confirmation that the clockwork concept worked as a physical system. Continuous motor rotation produced coordinated periodic strokes across the limb pairs without the timing collapsing under real assembly tolerances.

Because propulsion depends on the phase relationship between the limbs, this was more than a basic motion test. It validated the central kinematic idea behind the robot and gave us a stable platform for adding the final appendages and enclosure.

Mechanism Detail

Packaging the transmission

The completed mechanism required the gear train, linkages, supports, and limb pivots to coexist in a compact volume while remaining accessible enough to assemble and repair. These views show how motion is distributed from the motor through the stacked transmission and into the appendage linkages.

Build & Integration

From mechanism to complete system

Integrating the mechanism into a complete robot introduced new constraints that could not be solved independently. The electronics needed to remain dry, the housing had to fit the transmission, and the external shape needed to preserve a biologically meaningful carapace geometry for fluid testing.

The final assembly combined the clockwork mechanism, structural supports, waterproof electronics enclosure, control hardware, and appendages into one testable prototype. Reaching this stage required repeated full-system builds; several integration problems only became visible after the entire robot was assembled.

Control System

Light-activated operation

Direct access to the robot is limited during viscous-fluid testing, so the control system uses a light sensor as a simple non-invasive trigger. Applying a flashlight activates the motor; removing the light stops it.

Although the control behavior is simple, it solved an important system-level problem: how to start and stop an enclosed, submerged robot without compromising the test environment or waterproof housing.

Takeaways

This project gave me sustained experience across the full design-and-build cycle: translating a physical behavior into requirements, developing a mechanism, packaging electronics, fabricating parts, assembling the system, and discovering problems that only appear when every subsystem is combined.

It also reinforced my interest in engineering for unusual environments. The most rewarding work was not any single component, but balancing mechanism geometry, waterproofing, electronics, biological fidelity, and test constraints until the full system behaved as intended.

My direct involvement has concluded, but the project remains active at Swarthmore and continues to be developed by later student researchers.
