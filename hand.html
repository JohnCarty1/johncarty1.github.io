Gesture-Controlled Robotic Arm

November 2025 – January 2026

A collaborative embedded-systems project that translated hand orientation from a Bluetooth-connected glove into yaw, pitch, and gripping motion on a custom two-axis robotic arm.

RoleMechanical & controls integration

CAD, servo mechanism design, embedded logic, assembly, testing, and debugging.

SystemWireless gesture-controlled arm

ToolsCAD + embedded Python

Accelerometer processing, BLE UART, servo mapping, filtering, and mechanism design.

Overview

Hand rotation to arm movement

The system explored gesture-based control as a more intuitive alternative to a joystick. A glove-mounted sensor board measured hand orientation, filtered the readings, and transmitted commands wirelessly to the arm.

The receiving board converted those commands into yaw, pitch, and claw positions. The final demonstration therefore depended on reliable interaction between sensing, wireless communication, embedded software, servo control, and the mechanical design.

Mechanical System

Two-axis arm and servo-driven claw

One TowerPro MG92B servo in the base controlled yaw, while a second controlled pitch through the upper joint. A removable shaft simplified assembly and made the pitch mechanism easier to service. A smaller 9 g servo actuated the claw through a geared pincer arrangement.

Interactive CAD model of the completed arm and gripper mechanism.

Integrated Demonstration

From glove input to physical motion

The demonstration begins with the glove and arm pairing over Bluetooth. Once connected, hand rotation is mapped into arm movement while a separate input controls the gripper.

This was the central milestone of the project: combining the glove input, wireless link, control logic, and servo-driven mechanism into a responsive system rather than validating each subsystem only in isolation.

Software Control

Accelerometer-based gesture mapping

A three-axis accelerometer estimated glove pitch and roll. An exponential moving average reduced noise before the orientation values and grip state were sent over a BLE UART connection.

On the arm, the receiver parsed each packet and mapped the filtered angles into safe servo ranges. Keeping the sensing and actuation logic separate made the system easier to test and helped isolate communication, calibration, and mechanical issues.

Sensor board logic

ax, ay, az = read_accelerometer()

pitch = atan2(ay, sqrt(ax*ax + az*az))
roll  = atan2(ax, sqrt(ay*ay + az*az))

pitch_filtered = alpha * pitch + (1 - alpha) * pitch_filtered
roll_filtered  = alpha * roll  + (1 - alpha) * roll_filtered

send_ble_packet(roll_filtered, pitch_filtered, grip_state)

Receiver board logic

roll, pitch, grip = read_ble_packet()

yaw_servo.angle   = map_roll_to_servo(roll)
pitch_servo.angle = map_pitch_to_servo(pitch)

if grip:
    claw_servo.angle = closed_position
else:
    claw_servo.angle = open_position

Takeaways

This project gave me practical experience integrating sensing, wireless communication, embedded control, and mechanical actuation into one working device. It reinforced that a mechatronic system is only as useful as the interfaces between its subsystems.

It was also an effective introduction to embedded controls that now informs my reaction wheel attitude-control work, where the same themes of sensing, filtering, actuator commands, and physical response appear in a more spacecraft-oriented setting.
