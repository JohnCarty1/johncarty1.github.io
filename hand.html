Gesture-Controlled Robotic Arm

Nov 2025 - Jan 2026

A collaborative embedded systems project focused on translating hand gestures from a Bluetooth-connected glove into pitch, yaw, and gripping motion on a custom two-degree-of-freedom robotic arm.

Overview

Hand Rotation To Arm Movement

This project explored gesture-based control as a more intuitive way to operate a robotic arm. Instead of using a joystick or direct button controls, the system used a glove-mounted sensor board to read hand orientation and wirelessly send commands to the arm.

The final system connected sensing, Bluetooth communication, embedded control, and servo-driven mechanical motion. The arm could respond to the user's hand movements through yaw, pitch, and claw actuation.

Model

Robotic Arm Model

The arm used two primary joints for orientation control. One servo mounted in the base controlled yaw, while a second servo mounted above the base controlled pitch. A smaller servo actuated the claw, using a geared pincer mechanism to open and close the gripper.

Demonstration

The video shows the robotic arm responding to gesture input from the glove. You can see in the beginning the glove and arm pair to one another via Bluetooth. Hand motion is translated into movement commands, allowing the user to control the arm's orientation and gripping behavior.

This demonstration was the central milestone of the project: integrating the glove input, wireless communication, control logic, and servo-driven mechanism into a responsive working system.

Mechanical Design

Two-Axis Arm And Servo-Driven Claw

Mechanically, the arm was built around two main degrees of freedom. A TowerPro MG92B servo in the base controlled yaw, while another MG92B servo controlled pitch through the upper joint. The pitch joint used a removable shaft to make assembly and disassembly easier.

The claw was driven by a smaller 9g servo. Its motion was transmitted through a spur gear and shaft arrangement, allowing one motor to produce the pincer movement needed for gripping.

Software Control

Accelerometer-Based Gesture Mapping

The glove used a 3-axis accelerometer to estimate hand orientation. Acceleration values were converted into pitch and roll angles, smoothed with an exponential moving average filter, and sent over a BLE UART connection to the receiver board on the arm.

On the receiver side, incoming angle values were parsed and mapped to servo positions. Separate button states controlled whether the claw was closed and whether the glove was actively sending commands.

Sensor board logic

ax, ay, az = read_accelerometer()

pitch = atan2(ay, sqrt(ax*ax + az*az))
roll = atan2(ax, sqrt(ay*ay + az*az))

pitch_filtered = alpha * pitch + (1 - alpha) * pitch_filtered
roll_filtered = alpha * roll + (1 - alpha) * roll_filtered

send_ble_packet(roll_filtered, pitch_filtered, grip_state)

Receiver board logic

roll, pitch, grip = read_ble_packet()

yaw_servo.angle = map_roll_to_servo(roll)
pitch_servo.angle = map_pitch_to_servo(pitch)

if grip:
    claw_servo.angle = closed_position
else:
    claw_servo.angle = open_position

Takeaways

This project gave me hands-on experience integrating sensing, embedded control, wireless communication, and mechanical actuation into a single working system. It reinforced how much successful hardware depends on making separate subsystems communicate reliably and behave predictably together.


I value this project as an engaging introduction to embedded systems design. The skills I developed here continue to inform other areas of my work, especially my reaction wheel attitude control system, which I am currently developing.
