Overview

Turning internal momentum into attitude motion

Reaction wheels control spacecraft orientation by exchanging angular momentum with the vehicle. Accelerating the wheel produces an equal and opposite torque on the spacecraft body, allowing attitude changes without expelling propellant.

I began this project while studying analytical dynamics, using Euler's equations to model rigid-body rotation and then adding a reaction wheel as an internal actuator. The long-term goal is to carry that work into a physical platform where the simulated dynamics, actuator limits, sensor data, and controller can be compared directly.

Dynamics Foundation

Modeling rotational response

The simulation tracks the coupled motion of the spacecraft body and reaction wheel through angular momentum and Euler's rigid-body equations. It provides a way to explore how inertia distribution, initial rotation, wheel torque, and saturation affect the achievable response.

This stage is important because a small demonstrator can easily look active while still being poorly sized. The model helps determine whether a candidate wheel and motor can produce an observable, controllable attitude change rather than simply spinning rapidly inside the frame.

Engineering Trade Space

Wheel inertia

The wheel must store enough angular momentum to create a visible body response without becoming unnecessarily large or difficult to accelerate.

Motor capability

Available torque controls the attitude acceleration, while speed limits determine momentum storage and saturation behavior.

Structure and friction

The frame, bearings, wiring, and test support must minimize disturbances that could dominate the reaction-wheel torque.

Sensing and control

An IMU and closed-loop controller will be needed to move from open-loop demonstrations to measurable attitude regulation.

Current Direction

From simulation to hardware

The next phase is to finalize the mechanical architecture, choose a motor and wheel combination from the simulated torque and momentum requirements, and build the sensor and control stack around that hardware.

The first physical objective is a clean one-axis demonstration with well-documented open-loop response. From there, the platform can support feedback control, attitude estimation, and eventually a more complete multi-axis system.

Takeaway
