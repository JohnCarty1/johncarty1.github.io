Plasma Systems

May 2026 - Present

Experimental plasma systems research focused on high-temperature LaB6 cathode hardware for Swarthmore's LiPD plasma device, paired with particle-orbit simulations using WHAM magnetic-field data to support mirror fusion research at the University of Wisconsin-Madison.

Overview

Improving Plasma Generation

My work has focused on upgrading the electron source for the Little Plasma Device (LiPD) at Swarthmore College. LiPD is an approximately quarter-scale device with similar geometry and plasma behavior to WHAM, making it a useful local platform for studying physics relevant to the larger fusion experiment. The existing source used a hot tungsten filament, which produced limited electron beam current and plasma density. The goal of this project was to develop a more robust LaB6 cathode capable of producing substantially higher thermionic emission.

LaB6 is useful in plasma sources because it has a lower work function than tungsten, emits strongly at temperatures around 2000 K, and is more robust under plasma exposure than many conventional cathode materials. By increasing thermionic emission and plasma density in LiPD, the upgraded cathode allows us to study plasma behavior at Swarthmore in a regime more relevant to the questions our collaborators are asking about WHAM. The engineering challenge was not simply choosing the material, but designing a structure that could heat it effectively, support it mechanically, and survive inside the device.

Cathode Design

LaB6 Cathode Assembly

The cathode assembly was designed in SolidWorks around a central goal: heating the LaB6 emitter efficiently enough to support strong thermionic emission while still allowing the hardware to be mechanically supported, electrically connected, and integrated into the existing LiPD plasma device through a CF flange interface.

The design had to function as a coupled thermal, electrical, and mechanical system. Current enters through the LiPD connector, passes through custom copper and molybdenum components into a graphite contact holding the LaB6, then continues through the tungsten heater wrapped around the boron nitride sleeve. The heat shield also serves a dual role by reflecting radiative heat back toward the emitter while helping complete the electrical return path.

Electrical path: LiPD mount → copper interface → molybdenum support rod → graphite contact / LaB6 emitter → tungsten heater → molybdenum return rod → heat shield → isolated copper return interface.

The heater geometry was constrained by the available 50 A, 20 V power supply. To draw the full current and deliver the maximum 1000 W of heater power, the total circuit resistance needed to be 0.4 Ω. This meant the heater length, cross-sectional area, material resistivity, and contact geometry had to be designed around both electrical resistance and heat transfer.

Because the same parts often served multiple thermal, electrical, and mechanical functions, the designs could not be developed with only one aspect of the project in mind. At temperatures required for emission, materials are prone to melting, sublimation, softening, and many other problems that would destroy the entire assembly if not accounted for properly. Support rods, contacts, insulation, shielding, and interfaces all affected whether the cathode could heat efficiently, avoid electrical shorts, survive installation, and operate inside the device.

Design Evolution

From Disk To Cylindrical Emitter Concepts

The cathode design began with disk-shaped LaB6 emitter concepts. These early designs helped define the basic constraints of the project: the cathode needed to be mechanically supported, electrically heated, and thermally isolated well enough to reach the temperature range required for strong thermionic emission.

As the design developed, we shifted toward a cylindrical LaB6 geometry. The primary advantage was thermal: a cylindrical sleeve made it possible to wrap tungsten heater wire around the emitter region, increasing the contact area between the heater and LaB6. This geometry was the first design in our thermal simulations to produce temperature readings above 2000 K, making it a major turning point in the project.

The cylindrical concept was also motivated by prior LaB6 cathode work showing that, after a discharge is established, plasma bombardment can help maintain the cathode emission temperature even after heater power is reduced or removed. This made the cylindrical emitter especially interesting because it connected the mechanical geometry of the cathode to the plasma behavior it was intended to produce.

Thermal Analysis

Designing For 2000 K Operation

Using SolidWorks FEA, thermal analysis was performed to evaluate whether the cathode assembly could bring the LaB6 emitter into the temperature range needed for strong thermionic emission. Additionally, it was crucial to determine if any materials were exceeding their maximum working temperatures. The analysis connected the electrical design of the heater to the physical geometry of the assembly: the heater had to deliver enough power to the emitter while support rods, contacts, shielding, and surrounding hardware introduced possible heat-loss paths.

The most important design target was reaching approximately 2000 K near the LaB6 source. Thermal simulation was used to compare whether different geometries could concentrate heat at the emitter while keeping the surrounding structure from drawing too much heat away. This method of analysis led to the realization that a disk-shaped emitter could not be sufficiently heated within our assembly. The cylindrical heater concept became especially important because it improved thermal contact with the emitter region and produced the first simulation results above 2000 K.

Results

Experimental Impact

The upgraded cathode assembly demonstrated sustained high-temperature operation and contributed to a plasma density increase of roughly two orders of magnitude compared with the previous configuration.

This was the most important result of the project for me: the CAD, thermal analysis, material selection, and fabrication work connected directly to a measurable improvement in plasma performance.

Modeling

Particle Orbit Simulations

Alongside the cathode hardware, I have been working on charged-particle orbit simulations in magnetic mirror fields using actual WHAM magnetic-field data. The model tracks how ions move through the confinement geometry, including whether they remain trapped, pass through the mirror, or are lost due to pitch angle, energy, orbit size, or field geometry.

A primary component of the work I typically gravitate towards is an opportunity to tackle hardware design and fabrication challenges. But this project has added a computational depth to my work that I value: instead of treating simulation as separate from design, I am using it to understand the same physics that matters to the device. That is what makes this project especially meaningful to me. The orbit work connects directly to questions our WHAM collaborators care about as they improve plasma confinement and fusion-device performance. The appealing aspect of this work is that the simulations we conduct can be easily modified to accurately portray a multitude of systems from LiPD, to WHAM, to solar winds.
