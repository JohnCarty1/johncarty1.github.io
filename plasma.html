Overview

Building a Higher-Performance Electron Source

My work has focused on upgrading the electron source for the Little Plasma Device (LiPD) at Swarthmore College. LiPD is an approximately quarter-scale device that shares important geometric and operating characteristics with WHAM, making it a useful local platform for studying physics relevant to the larger fusion experiment. The existing source used a hot tungsten filament, which produced limited electron beam current and plasma density. The goal of this project was to develop a more robust LaB6 cathode capable of producing substantially greater thermionic emission.

LaB6 is useful in plasma sources because it has a lower work function than tungsten and emits strongly at temperatures around 2000 K. By increasing thermionic emission and plasma density in LiPD, the upgraded cathode allows us to study plasma behavior in a regime more relevant to the questions our collaborators are asking about WHAM. The engineering challenge was not simply choosing the material, but designing a structure that could heat it effectively, support it mechanically, and survive inside the device.

Cathode Design

LaB6 Cathode Assembly

The cathode assembly was designed in SolidWorks around a central goal: heating the LaB6 emitter efficiently enough to support strong thermionic emission while still allowing the hardware to be mechanically supported, electrically connected, and integrated into the existing LiPD plasma device through a CF flange interface.

The design had to function as a coupled thermal, electrical, and mechanical system. Current enters through the LiPD connector, passes through custom copper and molybdenum components into a graphite contact holding the LaB6, then continues through the tungsten heater wrapped around the boron nitride sleeve. The heat shield also serves a dual role by reflecting radiative heat back toward the emitter while helping complete the electrical return path.

Electrical path: LiPD mount → copper interface → molybdenum support rod → graphite contact / LaB6 emitter → tungsten heater → molybdenum return rod → heat shield → isolated copper return interface.

The heater geometry was constrained by the available 50 A, 20 V power supply. To draw the full current and deliver the maximum 1000 W of heater power, the total circuit resistance needed to be 0.4 Ω. This meant the heater length, cross-sectional area, material resistivity, and contact geometry had to be designed around both electrical resistance and heat transfer.

Because many components served multiple thermal, electrical, and mechanical functions, material selection and integration were critical. At emission temperatures, parts can melt, sublime, soften, create heat-loss paths, or introduce electrical failure points. Each support rod, contact, insulator, shield, and interface therefore had to be evaluated as part of the complete assembly so the cathode could heat efficiently, avoid shorts, survive installation, and operate reliably inside LiPD.

Design Evolution

From Disk to Cylindrical Emitters

The cathode design began with disk-shaped LaB6 emitter concepts. These early designs helped define the project's basic constraints: the emitter needed mechanical support, electrical heating, and enough thermal isolation to reach the temperature range required for strong thermionic emission.

As the design developed, we shifted toward a cylindrical LaB6 geometry. A cylindrical sleeve allowed tungsten heater wire to wrap around the emitter region, increasing the contact area between the heater and LaB6. It was the first geometry in our thermal simulations to produce temperatures above 2000 K and became a major turning point in the project.

Prior LaB6 cathode work also suggested that, after a discharge is established, plasma bombardment can help maintain the emission temperature even after heater power is reduced. This made the cylindrical emitter especially interesting because it connected the cathode's mechanical geometry to the plasma behavior it was intended to produce.

Thermal Analysis

Designing for 2000 K Operation

Using SolidWorks FEA, I evaluated whether the assembly could bring the LaB6 emitter into the temperature range needed for strong thermionic emission without pushing surrounding materials beyond their operating limits. The heater had to deliver sufficient power to the emitter while support rods, contacts, shielding, and surrounding hardware created competing heat-loss paths.

The central design target was approximately 2000 K near the LaB6 source. The disk-shaped emitter could not be heated sufficiently within the assembly, while the cylindrical heater improved thermal contact and produced the first simulation results above 2000 K.

Commissioning & High-Current Operation

Sustained Operation at the Full Design Current

On July 17, 2026, the completed LaB6 cathode produced its first visible plasma at 40 A after fabrication, assembly, and installation in LiPD. Commissioning then progressed to sustained operation at the full 50 A heater-current design point, with a bright argon discharge visible through the axial and exterior viewports.

Operation at the design current confirmed that the heater circuit, refractory assembly, electrical contacts, support structure, and vacuum interface could function together under the intended load while sustaining the discharge. During high-current runs, we also positioned an axial Langmuir probe for voltage-current sweeps and plasma-column characterization while varying source and device conditions.
